Rules/Calculations

This section covers all of the D&D rules and calculations that your program must implement. Other sections may refer to these requirements.

Ability Scores: Rolling

The user can choose to roll their ability scores. Rolling each score uses the following steps:

  • Roll four six-sided dice
  • Remove the die with the lowest value (in case of a tie, only remove one die)
  • Sum the remaining three dice to determine the ability score

This method produces scores between 3 and 18 (inclusive). This allows for lower and higher scores than a player could normally get through point buy, but the player has less control over their scores. Our program is also assigning the scores in the order they were rolled rather than letting the player assign them.

Ability Scores: Point Buy

The user can choose to use D&D 5e's point buy system to determine their ability scores. The point buy system has the following rules:

  • The user has a budget of 27 points to spend on their ability scores.

A note about the cleric's AC: we add 15 to either the cleric's dexterity modifier or 2, whichever is less. This limits the cleric's dexterity modifier to +2 or less for the purpose of calculating their armor class. You can use the Math.min function to take the lower of two numbers.

Hit Points

A character's hit points can be calculated by rolling their hit die or taking an average value for each level. A hit die is written as "d" followed by the number of sides on the die. For example, "d12" refers to a twelve-sided die. To calculate a character's hit points:

  • Use the maximum value (the number of sides on the die) for first level, regardless of whether you are rolling or taking the average.
  • For each level after first:
    • If rolling, then roll the hit die and add the character's constitution modifier to the result
    • If taking the average, then use 1 + half the die size + the character's constitution modifier
    • A character gains a minimum of 1 hit point per level. If the constitution modifier is negative, then you need to make sure the result for any given level is not less than 1. One way to do this is to use Math.max to take the larger value between 1 and the number you calculated in the previous step.

For example, let's calculate the hit points of a 3rd level fighter with a constitution modifier of -2. In the class table we can see that a fighter's hit die is d10.

  • Rolling (let's assume I roll a 2 and a 7 on a d10)
    • The fighter gains 8 hp at first level: 10 (max hit die value) plus -2 (con)
    • The fighter gains 1 hp at second level: 2 (number rolled on a d10) plus -2 (con) is less than 1, but a character must gain a minimum of 1 hp per level
    • The fighter gains 5 hp at second level: 7 (number rolled on a d10) plus -2 (con)
    • Total hp: 14
  • Average
    • The fighter gains 8 hp at first level: 10 (max hit die value) plus -2 (con)
    • The fighter gains 4 hp at second and third level: 6 (half hit die plus one) plus -2 (con)
    • Total hp: 16

The I/O requirements work just like a guided project, and they will be evaluated by the same test program. Your program should also include prompts for information and any additional text that you think would make it easier to use. It's up to you how much of this to include, what exactly to write, and how to format it. The tests will not check these prompts.

Your program must accept the following user input:

  • (String) player's name
  • (String) character's name
  • (String) character's ancestry
  • (String) character's class
  • (int) character's level
  • (int) either "1" or "2" to indicate whether the user wishes to (1) roll their hit points or (2) use average hit points
  • (int) either "1" or "2" to indicate whether the user wishes to generate their scores with (1) point buy or (2) dice roll
