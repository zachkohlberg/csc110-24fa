Description

CSC110 is an introduction to programming using the Java programming language and the Jshell REPL. The primary focus of this class is developing problem-solving skills and a general knowledge of programming that will be useful to you in most programming languages. While we will spend a lot of time learning about Java and how to write good code, writing code isn't our ultimate goal. The most important skill you can learn in this class is how to think like a programmer. Once you've mastered that, writing code is surprisingly easy.

This syllabus also covers any sections of CSC110L that I am teaching this semester. Policies specific to CSC110L are in the lab section of this syllabus. If you are taking the lab with a different instructor, then refer to their lab syllabus.

Objectives

CSC110 and CSC110L share objectives. Upon successful completion of both courses, students should be able to:

  • Discuss the challenges inherent in communicating with (programming) a (non-reasoning) computer.
  • Break a complex problem down into smaller more manageable components.
  • Provide step-by-step instructions to solve small computational problems.
  • Using at least one programming language, write examples of and solve problems in programs with the following basic programming constructs and tools:
    • Mathematical expressions
    • Conditional expressions
    • Simple iterative statements
    • Simple functions
    • Simple data structures, such as arrays, lists, and strings
  • Discuss the importance of commenting and good code structure/style

Assignments

Projects

I will assign several projects during the semester, and I've created a separate website covering the details of these projects. Each project falls into one of the following categories:

  • A Guided Project provides step-by-step instructions for writing a program. These steps won't tell you exactly what to code, but they break the program into a series of smaller problems that you can solve one at a time.
  • An Expanded Project builds on previous Guided Projects to create a more complete program. It is significantly more complex than a Guided Project, and you will not be given step-by-step directions for completing each task. You also have the option to propose an alternative version of the assignment.
  • A Self-Directed Project requires you to design your own program and submit a proposal describing it. Self-Directed Projects are optional and can be used to replace any of the other projects.

Project Submission

All projects must be submitted on Blackboard, and most projects must pass a set of tests to be considered complete. To make it easier for me to check new submissions frequently, I require projects to be zipped in a particular format. I will provide a program that checks whether your project passes these tests and then zips your project in the correct format.

If a project submission is improperly zipped, is missing any tasks/deliverables, or does not pass the required test cases, then that submission will be marked as incomplete. You may resubmit the project as long as you follow the restrictions in the deadlines section below.

Deadlines

I don't like assigning deadlines for assignments unless there is a compelling reason that I need your work by a particular time or need to otherwise limit submissions. If I do set a deadline or other restriction, then I will explain why.

The following restrictions apply to project submissions:

  • You are allowed to submit Guided Project assignments as often as you like until 11:59 PM ET on Sunday, December 8th.
  • You can submit up to one proposal for a Self-Directed Project or an alternative Expanded Project every week.
  • You can submit up to one Self-Directed Project or Expanded Project (I'll refer to these as the open-ended projects) each week.
  • If a project requires me to approve a proposal, then you cannot submit that project until the week after you submit the proposal.
  • The cutoff for weekly proposal/project submissions is 11:59 PM ET on Sunday. If you submit after this time, then it will count as your submission for the following week. The final weekly deadline is 11:59 PM ET on Sunday, December 8th.
  • I will not accept any late submissions and will strictly enforce all deadlines. The deadline for Guided Projects and final weekly deadline for open-ended projects is your last opportunity to submit work for this class.

My reasons for these restrictions:

  • The Guided Project assignments can be fully checked by my test program and a couple of scripts, so they take little time for me to review. I leave myself a week at the end of the semester to determine final grades, review projects one last time, and resolve academic integrity cases.
  • I chose to go with weekly project/proposal submissions instead of project-specific deadlines to keep the open-ended projects more flexible. The limit of one per week ensures I don't get flooded with too many late in the semester. I aim to review them within a week of the cut-off so you can have timely feedback.
  • I give specific times and enforce deadlines strictly to ensure I handle assignment submissions consistently and fairly. It doesn't matter how close you are to a missed deadline or what excuse you offer. You either submit the assignment on time or arrange for an extension (see below); otherwise, I will not accept the assignment.

Grading

This class uses a labor-based grading system. I don't expect most students to be familiar with labor-based grading, so before I get into our particular version, I'd like to introduce the concept and explain why we're using it.

What is Labor-Based Grading?

Labor-based grading and contract grading are approaches to grading that try to emphasize the work you put into a class over the outcomes of that work. This grading system specifies what work you need to complete to earn a particular final grade, and individual assignments are not graded. You receive a final grade based on what work you complete rather than a weighted average of grades from assignments and exams.

There are plenty of articles online about labor-based grading if you want to read about it in more detail. Here's one that was easy to find and that I think explains the concept well: https://cep.barnard.edu/labor-based-grading. This article also links to an example grade contract. My implementation will differ, but my goals are largely the same.

Why Labor-Based Grading?

The author of the example grade contract shown in the Bernard College webpage gives an excellent explanation of why they prefer contract grading over traditional grades. I recommend reading that if you want to hear more about the motivation behind labor-based grading. I'll try to summarize why I dislike traditional grading systems and think this system addresses some (but not all) of the problems with grades.

  • Grades are not a good source of feedback. Grades provide little information on their own compared to comments and other qualitative feedback.
  • Grades punish you for mistakes. Whether intended or not, low grades act as a punishment. Mistakes are a necessary part of the learning process, and you should not be penalized for them. Giving lower grades to indicate mistakes or areas for improvement is counterproductive.
  • A laber-based grading system is more transparent than a weighted average. If you want a particular grade, then you know exactly which assignments you must complete for that grade.

The Grading System

Previous versions of this syllabus required you to choose a final grade and submit a grade contract with the terms for that grade. After trying this a few times, I don't think requiring everyone to submit their own contract is worth the trouble. Instead, I've listed each possible grade along with the required work to earn that grade. At the end of the semester, you will receive the highest final grade for which you qualify. You qualify for a grade if you meet every requirement.

  • For an A:
    • Complete all Guided Project assignments (1.1, 1.2, 2.1, 2.2)
    • Complete two Expanded Project assignments (1.3, 2.3)
  • For a B:
    • Complete all Guided Project assignments (1.1, 1.2, 2.1, 2.2)
    • Complete one Expanded Project assignment (1.3, 2.3)
  • For a C:
    • Complete all Guided Project assignments (1.1, 1.2, 2.1, 2.2)
  • For a D:
    • Complete three of the four Guided Project assignments (1.1, 1.2, 2.1, 2.2)
  • For an F:
    • Don't meet the requirements for any of the above grades

The Self-Directed Project can be used to replace an Expanded Project or a pair of Guided Projects. You can complete multiple Self-Directed Projects to replace additional assignments.

FAQ

  • If I'm not graded on an assignment, does that mean I don't need to put any effort into it? I review assignments and check that they were completed as directed. Not receiving a grade on your assignment doesn't mean you don't have to do any work or put effort into your work. You should still expect these assignments to challenge you and require a significant amount of work.
  • Why can't I get an A+? QU's grading system does not include an A+. The highest grade you can earn in any QU class is an A.
  • Why can't I get an A-, B+, B-, C+, or C-? I don't see a reason to include an option for every in-between grade. An A is the highest grade you can earn, a B is less demanding but good grade, and a C represents the minimum requirements to pass this class and take more CSC/SER courses.
  • Is there extra credit? There is no extra credit, but there are multiple ways to meet the requirements for any grade contract. You can replace anything with a Self-Directed Project, and you can propose alternative versions of Expanded Projects.
  • Can I get partial credit on an assignment? There is no partial credit for late or incomplete assignments. You must complete the entire assignment, submit it on time, and submit it in the correct format to receive credit for it. If you're having trouble, then please ask me or a tutor for help.
  • Are there exams? Graded exams, quizzes, and similar assessments don't work with this grading system. There are no exams, not even a final exam. However, I may give ungraded quizzes as a way for you to check your understanding of topics we've covered.

If you have questions about the grading system that aren't answered here, then please don't hesitate to ask me about it! I want you to feel comfortable with the grading system and understand what's required to earn the grade you want.

Academic Integrity (AI) Policy

Here is a link to Quinnipiac University's Academic Integrity (AI) policy: https://catalog.qu.edu/university-policies/academic-integrity-policy/. Permission to Share and Discuss Work

I consider it important that you can freely discuss assignments with other students. Your peers can be an excellent source of help and feedback, and your own insights and feedback can greatly help other students. I don't want the school's or my AI policies to discourage you from talking to anyone else about your work. Therefore, this section serves as my explicit written permission to do the following:

  • You may share work you create for this class with anybody, including your classmates, and publicly post this work online.
  • You may discuss assignments from this class with anybody. You may ask anybody for help with these assignments and view their work if they are willing to share it with you.

In summary, you own all the work you do for this class, and that means you decide what to do with it. However, I need to clarify that this permission does not extend to the following actions, all of which directly violate the school's AI policy:

  • You may not share work or post it publicly for the purpose of helping someone else violate the school's AI policy. This includes if somebody asks you for your work and informs you that they plan to copy it before you share the work with them. See the section on "Facilitation and Collusion" in the school's AI policy.
  • You may not copy your work from other people or from a large language model (an "artificial intelligence" program). I'll cover this in more detail in a later section.

I do not consider you responsible for anyone's misuse of material that you shared in good faith. I will always take your word regarding how you intended or expected your work to be used unless I have strong evidence that contradicts you.

What Counts as Plagiarism?

First and most important: in any situation where you have doubts or are unsure whether an action would violate this policy, ask me! It's always okay to ask whether you can do something.

This section exists for two reasons: to make it easier for someone who has genuine questions to determine whether an action qualifies as cheating, and to give me something to point to if somebody claims they didn't know their actions qualified as cheating. Going through a series of flimsy excuses is one of the most tedious parts of handling an AI violation, so I'd like to be thorough here and save myself some trouble later.

This will focus on plagiarism on programming assignments. I'll use the phrase "other work" to refer to any work that was created by somebody other than you or by an LLM (such as ChatGPT). The following actions will nearly always violate the AI policy:

  • Copying other work in whole or in part, whether by copying the file, copying and pasting the file's contents, or viewing the file and retyping the same information by hand. Modifying other work after copying it, whether to disguise the copying or "make it your own" (an excuse I've heard frequently) does not change the fact that it was copied. What matters is that you copied work, not how you copied it or what you did before or after copying it.
  • Paraphrasing other work in whole or in part. As far as I'm concerned, this is the same as copying. In my experience, some paraphrasing is often used to disguise that work was copied, and the claim that work was paraphrased is used as an excuse to deny having copied work.
  • Using other work as a reference when creating your own work. This normally results in copying or paraphrasing as described above, but I mention it because it's another common excuse used to explain evidence of copying.
  • Doing your work under the direction of another person. It's okay to talk to someone about your work while you're working, but it's not okay to let them dictate the code to you. If you're mostly writing what someone else tells you to write, then you are not doing your own work. Try talking to them about your problem, taking some notes, and then fixing the problem on your own.

Exceptions to the above:

  • You can use material that I intentionally provide you (such as my notes, starter code, assignment descriptions, or examples from class) with or without modification in your own work for this class. You don't need to document your use of such material because it is expected and obvious from context.
  • If you want to copy or paraphrase from another source, then you can as long as you do the following:
    • Discuss it with me first and get my permission to use the other work on the assignment.
    • Clearly state which outside sources you used and provide a reliable link or other means to access those sources.
    • Clearly show what was taken from the other source and what is your own original work.

Two general rules I use if someone appears to have plagiarized but claims they did not:

  • If large sections of your code are identical or nearly identical to existing code, then it isn't your work. If you are writing your own code and following the guidelines I provide in this policy, then you should always end up with significantly different code on all but the simplest programs.
  • If you don't understand your code and cannot explain how it works, then it isn't your work. You should not be using code that you don't understand, and you are not going to accidentally write a working program that solves a specific problem without some understanding of the code you're writing. If your code doesn't appear to be something a student realistically would have written and you cannot explain how it works, then I will not accept it for an assignment.

Retaking this Class and Self-Plagiarism

If you are retaking this class, then I expect you to complete every assignment from scratch without reusing any of your old work. I consider reusing work from the last time you took this class equivalent to reusing work you completed for a different class ("double-dipping" in QU's AI policy).

ChatGPT and LLMs

You're free to discuss assignments with LLMs such as ChatGPT, but I do not recommend using them for this class. While we might colloquially refer to an LLM as an "artificial intelligence," these tools are not intelligent and do not understand the conversation they are having with you. They are complex algorithms running on powerful hardware, and they are very good at one thing: using their training data to produce text that looks like something a human might write in the context of your conversation. LLMs are prone to hallucinating (making up false information), and they do not know when they are hallucinating. These tools are most useful in the hands of an expert who can critically evaluate the information generated by an LLM. You are most likely not an expert at this point.

As mentioned earlier, I treat material generated by an LLM the same as material created by another person for the purpose of this AI policy. It is not your work, and using this material in an assignment violates this policy.

If you are using ChatGPT for help with an assignment, then please save that conversation so you can share it with me or the AI Office if your work is called into question. This is an easy way to establish whether you used the tool appropriately, and if you cannot or will not provide that evidence then I will not give you the benefit of the doubt.

Consequences for AI Violations

Consequences for AI violations are determined either by joint resolution, case review, or a hearing. In a joint resolution, you and I must agree to any proposed consequences, and in a case review or hearing I will still be consulted about appropriate consequences. This section covers what I believe are appropriate consequences on a first offense for most AI violations.

If you plagiarize, cheat, or otherwise violate the AI policy on an assignment, then under no circumstances will I consider it appropriate to give you credit for the assignment. This is not a punishment for cheating, it is simply enforcing the requirements of the grading system. If you copied or cheated on even a small portion of the assignment, then you did not complete the entire assignment; you completed some of the assignment and then copied the remaining work. To give any credit, full or partial, would be a reward for cheating.

In addition to enforcing the normal penalty for incomplete work, I consider the following consequences to be appropriate:

  • If you cheat on one assignment, then your final grade should be reduced by one letter grade after accounting for the incomplete assignment. I will not allow you to redo the assignment, but you may replace the assignment with a Self-Directed Project if there is enough time remaining. If there isn't enough time to complete a Self-Directed Project, then I will not give you an extension.
  • If you cheat on multiple assignments, then you should receive a final grade of F. Depending on the extent or nature of this cheating (for example, if you cheat on more than two assignments), further penalties may be appropriate.
